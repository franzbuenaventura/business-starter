EXECUTIVE
 
SUMMARY
 
 
The
 
Executive
 
Summary
 
is
 
the
 
most
 
important
 
section
 
of
 
your
 
business
 
plan.
 
It's
 
often
 
the
 
first
 
(and
 
sometimes
 
the
 
only)
 
part
 
a
 
potential
 
investor,
 
lender,
 
or
 
partner
 
reads.
 
In
 
many
 
cases,
 
it
 
determines
 
whether
 
they'll
 
continue
 
reviewing
 
your
 
plan.
 
A
 
strong
 
Executive
 
Summary
 
is
 
clear,
 
concise,
 
confident,
 
and
 
compelling.
 
It
 
should
 
reflect
 
your
 
enthusiasm
 
for
 
your
 
idea
 
and
 
give
 
readers
 
a
 
reason
 
to
 
want
 
to
 
learn
 
more.
 
It
 
should
 
also
 
demonstrate
 
that
 
you
 
understand
 
how
 
your
 
business
 
will
 
operate
 
and
 
generate
 
revenue
 
-
 
not
 
just
 
the
 
idea
 
behind
 
it.
 
Write
 
this
 
section
 
last.
 
Once
 
you've
 
worked
 
through
 
the
 
rest
 
of
 
your
 
plan,
 
you'll
 
have
 
a
 
much
 
clearer
 
picture
 
of
 
your
 
business
 
concept
 
and
 
be
 
better
 
positioned
 
to
 
summarize
 
it
 
effectively.
 
 
Your
 
Executive
 
Summary
 
should
 
briefly
 
address
 
each
 
of
 
the
 
following:
 
 
1
 
Your
 
business
 
idea
 
-
 
a
 
one-
 
or
 
two-sentence
 
overview.
 
What
 
are
 
you
 
building,
 
and
 
why
 
does
 
it
 
matter?
 
2
 
Your
 
product
 
or
 
service
 
-
 
what
 
are
 
you
 
offering,
 
and
 
what
 
problem
 
does
 
it
 
solve
 
for
 
your
 
customers?
 
3
 
Your
 
business
 
model
 
-
 
how
 
will
 
your
 
business
 
make
 
money?
 
What
 
are
 
your
 
primary
 
revenue
 
streams?
 
4
 
Your
 
goals
 
-
 
where
 
do
 
you
 
expect
 
the
 
business
 
to
 
be
 
in
 
one
 
year?
 
Three
 
years?
 
Five
 
years?
 
5
 
Your
 
customer
 
acquisition
 
strategy
 
-
 
how
 
will
 
you
 
reach
 
and
 
attract
 
your
 
target
 
customers?
 
6
 
Your
 
target
 
market
 
-
 
who
 
are
 
your
 
ideal
 
customers,
 
and
 
why
 
are
 
they
 
the
 
right
 
audience
 
for
 
what
 
you're
 
offering?
 
7
 
Your
 
competition
 
and
 
your
 
edge
 
-
 
who
 
are
 
you
 
up
 
against,
 
and
 
what
 
sets
 
you
 
apart?
 
8
 
Your
 
team
 
-
 
who's
 
involved,
 
and
 
what
 
experience
 
or
 
skills
 
do
 
they
 
bring
 
that
 
will
 
help
 
the
 
business
 
succeed?
 
9
 
Your
 
financial
 
outlook
 
and
 
key
 
assumptions
 
-
 
if
 
you're
 
seeking
 
financing,
 
be
 
specific:
 
how
 
much
 
do
 
you
 
need,
 
how
 
do
 
you
 
plan
 
to
 
use
 
it,
 
and
 
how
 
will
 
it
 
help
 
the
 
business
 
grow
 
and
 
become
 
profitable?
 
 
Limit
 
your
 
Executive
 
Summary
 
to
 
one
 
or
 
two
 
pages.
 
When
 
someone
 
finishes
 
reading
 
it,
 
they
 
should
 
understand
 
your
 
business
 
concept,
 
believe
 
it
 
has
 
real
 
potential,
 
and
 
want
 
to
 
keep
 
reading.
 
Even
 
at
 
an
 
early
 
stage,
 
include
 
any
 
evidence
 
of
 
traction
 
-
 
such
 
as
 
early
 
sales,
 
customer
 
interest,
 
partnerships,
 
or
 
market
 
validation.
