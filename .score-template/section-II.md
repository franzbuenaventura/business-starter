INSTRUCTIONS:
 
COMPANY
 
DESCRIPTION
 
 
This
 
section
 
gives
 
readers
 
a
 
clear
 
picture
 
of
 
what
 
your
 
business
 
is,
 
what
 
it
 
stands
 
for,
 
and
 
how
 
it's
 
structured.
 
Cover
 
each
 
of
 
the
 
following
 
elements:
 
1.
 
Mission
 
Statement
 
Your
 
mission
 
statement
 
is
 
a
 
concise
 
explanation
 
of
 
why
 
your
 
business
 
exists
 
-
 
what
 
it
 
does,
 
who
 
it
 
serves,
 
and
 
what
 
makes
 
it
 
distinctive.
 
It
 
can
 
be
 
as
 
brief
 
as
 
a
 
single
 
tagline:
 
"MoreDough
 
is
 
an
 
app
 
that
 
helps
 
consumers
 
manage
 
their
 
personal
 
finances
 
in
 
a
 
fun,
 
convenient
 
way."
 
Or
 
a
 
bit
 
more
 
descriptive:
 
"Doggie
 
Tales
 
is
 
a
 
dog
 
daycare
 
and
 
grooming
 
salon
 
specializing
 
in
 
convenient
 
services
 
for
 
urban
 
pet
 
lovers.
 
Our
 
mission
 
is
 
to
 
provide
 
safety,
 
service,
 
and
 
a
 
welcoming
 
atmosphere
 
-
 
helping
 
busy
 
dog
 
owners
 
spend
 
less
 
time
 
on
 
logistics
 
and
 
more
 
time
 
enjoying
 
their
 
pet."
 
Aim
 
for
 
one
 
to
 
two
 
sentences.
 
Clear
 
and
 
specific
 
beats
 
clever
 
and
 
vague.
 
2.
 
Philosophy
 
and
 
Vision
 
Philosophy
 
is
 
about
 
your
 
values
 
-
 
the
 
principles
 
your
 
business
 
operates
 
by.
 
Think
 
about
 
what
 
matters
 
most
 
to
 
how
 
you
 
work:
 
honesty,
 
innovation,
 
community,
 
sustainability,
 
customer
 
focus.
 
What
 
will
 
guide
 
your
 
decisions
 
when
 
things
 
get
 
hard?
 
 
Vision
 
is
 
your
 
long-term
 
ambition.
 
Where
 
do
 
you
 
want
 
this
 
business
 
to
 
go?
 
A
 
local
 
dog
 
daycare
 
might
 
aspire
 
to
 
become
 
a
 
regional
 
brand,
 
a
 
national
 
franchise,
 
or
 
be
 
acquired
 
by
 
a
 
larger
 
corporation.
 
A
 
software
 
startup
 
might
 
aim
 
to
 
become
 
the
 
go-to
 
platform
 
in
 
its
 
category.
 
Be
 
specific
 
about
 
what
 
success
 
looks
 
like
 
on
 
a
 
longer
 
horizon.
 
3.
 
Goals
 
and
 
Milestones
 
Outline
 
both
 
your
 
short-term
 
and
 
long-term
 
goals,
 
and
 
identify
 
the
 
milestones
 
you'll
 
use
 
to
 
measure
 
progress.
 
Vague
 
goals
 
are
 
hard
 
to
 
act
 
on;
 
the
 
more
 
specific,
 
the
 
better.
 
For
 
example,
 
if
 
opening
 
a
 
second
 
location
 
is
 
a
 
long-term
 
goal,
 
your
 
milestones
 
might
 
include
 
hitting
 
a
 
specific
 
monthly
 
revenue
 
target
 
or
 
securing
 
a
 
set
 
number
 
of
 
clients
 
in
 
a
 
new
 
market.
 
Concrete
 
benchmarks
 
keep
 
you
 
accountable
 
and
 
help
 
readers
 
assess
 
whether
 
your
 
plan
 
is
 
realistic.
 
4.
 
Target
 
Market
 
You'll
 
address
 
this
 
more
 
in-depth
 
in
 
the
 
Marketing
 
Plan
 
section.
 
Here,
 
briefly
 
describe
 
who
 
your
 
ideal
 
customers
 
are,
 
who
 
you're
 
building
 
this
 
company
 
for,
 
and
 
why
 
they
 
want
 
or
 
need
 
what
 
you're
 
offering.
 
5.
 
Industry
 
Overview
 
Describe
 
the
 
industry
 
your
 
business
 
is
 
in
 
and
 
where
 
it
 
fits
 
within
 
it.
 
Is
 
the
 
industry
 
growing,
 
stable,
 
or
 
going
 
through
 
significant
 
change?
 
What
 
trends
 
are
 
shaping
 
its
 
future,
 
and
 
how
 
does
 
your
 
business
 
take
 
advantage
 
of
 
them?
 
Who
 
are
 
your
 
competitors,
 
and
 
what
 
will
 
it
 
take
 
to
 
outperform
 
them?
 
Investors
 
and
 
lenders
 
want
 
to
 
see
 
that
 
you
 
understand
 
the
 
competitive
 
landscape
 
you're
 
walking
 
into.Depending
 
on
 
the
 
type
 
of
 
business
 
you're
 
starting,
 
it's
 
critical
 
to
 
understand
 
that
 
your
 
competitors
 
could
 
be
 
local,
 
regional,
 
national,
 
or
 
even
 
global.
 
6.
 
Legal
 
Structure
 
Identify
 
your
 
business
 
structure:
 
sole
 
proprietorship,
 
LLC,
 
partnership,
 
or
 
corporation.
 
Briefly
 
explain
 
why
 
you
 
chose
 
it.
 
Tax
 
treatment,
 
liability
 
protection,
 
and
 
flexibility
 
for
 
future
 
investment
 
are
 
common
 
factors.
 
You
 
may
 
want
 
to
 
consult
 
with
 
your
 
accountant
 
before
 
choosing
 
a
 
business
 
structure.If
 
your
 
business
 
has
 
multiple
 
owners,
 
describe
 
how
 
ownership
 
is
 
divided.
 
If
 
you
 
have
 
investors,
 
note
 
their
 
ownership
 
percentage.
 
This
 
information
 
is
 
crucial
 
to
 
lenders
 
and
 
investors
 
when
 
evaluating
 
the
 
financial
 
and
 
governance
 
side
 
of
 
your
 
business.
 
 
By
 
the
 
end
 
of

what

(mission
 
and

(goals),
 
who

(market
 
and

(business
